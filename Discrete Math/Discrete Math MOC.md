# Discrete Math MOC

Organized by topic, not by source — one place to find a concept as understood across both Epp (5th ed., confirmed against the actual table of contents) and MIT 6.1200J.

## How to use this MOC
- Literature notes stay source-specific, titled by content, not chapter number.
- Permanent notes are topic-specific and source-agnostic. One concept, one note, even when two sources teach it.
- When a second source adds a useful angle, it enriches the existing permanent note rather than spawning a duplicate.
- Some sections split across two topic headings because the book's chapter boundaries do not line up with clean topic boundaries — this is intentional, not an error.
- A heading with only Epp or only MIT sources is expected in a couple of places — real gaps between the two sources, not notes you forgot to write.

## Study plan
- Add a literature note for each section as it's completed, titled by content — never by chapter number alone.
- Ch4 and Ch8 both feed Number Theory — don't be surprised the RSA/modular arithmetic material shows up under Relations (Ch8) rather than the proof-and-number-theory chapter (Ch4). That's how this edition actually organizes it.
- Ch5 and Ch9 both split across two headings — this is intentional, not a mapping error. Ch5's induction sections are proof technique; its sequences/correctness sections are closer to recurrences. Ch9's early sections are pure counting; its last two sections are genuinely probability.
- Ch6.4 (Russell's Paradox, the Halting Problem) is worth flagging as a strong permanent-note candidate — it's the one place in this book where set theory foundations directly touch computability, which is unusually good territory for a cross-link into your dev curriculum.
- Keep this page as the browsing layer, not a storage layer.
